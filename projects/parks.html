Thinking of how to transform the traditional poster into a functional pamphlet

category
research
print design
role
graphic design 1 project
date
january 2018 - april 2018

about

For this project, we were required to create a series of posters for three National Parks with an icon system. I decided I wanted to create posters that were not only beautiful to look at, but also practical. These posters would fulfill several different user needs and be an information source that could be used at all time, from planning the trip to returning home. I designed this pamphlet system to show information on one side and a more traditional poster on the other. This set would be held together by a strict system of hierarchy and color as well as a strong grid. Although we only had to design three, I wanted to make sure these could be applied to any of the other National Parks as well.

icon system

As I began working on the poster set, my first focus was developing the icons. The style I chose here would influence the design of poster as a whole. With that I knew I wanted a simpler style in black and white to place the focus on the imagery that would bolster the set. When choosing what to depict, I decided to create symbols that represented what could be done at the parks, as that is what would impact people as they decided what park to visit. These icons would be shared across the posters so the activities could be easily compared. Below are the final icons I designed.

These icons highlighted several activities and aspects of the park. Knowing whether or not a park allows pets is super important, so I made one of my icons represent pets being allowed. From left to right these icons represented: bird watching, camping, fishing, hiking, pets allowed, and water activities.

designing the posters

With the idea of a pamphlet, I knew I needed to split the page into several sections. With a required size of 20 inches by 32 incehs and the desire to make it pocket-sized, I created 3 fold lines to split the paper into four sections.

The next part was to decide what information I would include on my poster. Just like my icons, I wanted to make sure to get across the most important information about the park. Through brainstorming and talking with friends, what would be important to plan the trip and once at the park itself. The concensus was that knowing about the park and the activities is important, but also ways to learn more information anf how to contact the park (both for planning and in case of emergencies). With the information decided I went to designing the informational side of the posters.

On the informational side I made sure to ionclude photography, information about the park and the activities, as well as importnat contact information, like guest services and emergency information.

As for the front, this would serve as a selling point and a strong visual that could be hung up and serve as a souvenir . Searching through creative commons images, I searched for an image that capture the beauty and essence of each park. These images were then centered on the back with the name of the park and location. The same imagery was used on informational side's first and last panel. Below are the final designs for the imagery part of the pamphelet.

The imagery side focused on one strong visuals and minimum amounts of text. My layout was inspired by Ansel Adams prints I had seen before.

reflection

Although this was a print project, I thought about it from a user experience perspective. I highly considered what information the user needed and allowed them to show/hide information by folding the pamphlet. The pamphlet could support the user throughout the journey of going to National Park. For these reasons, this series is one of my favorite print pieces I have worked on. This project allowed me to use my UX knowledge while also working on grids and typography systems for print.
